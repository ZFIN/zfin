PhD fellowship

(University of Birmingham, UK)

Cis-regulatory interactions in vertebrate developmental gene expression Many developmental abnormalities and pathological processes are resulting from the misregulation of gene expression. Such can occur due to mutations and rearrangements of long-range cis-regulatory modules. The target genes involved in disorders caused by mutations in long range elements are difficult to identify, because the mechanisms that provide specificity to long range interaction between enhancers and their cognate promoters are poorly understood. The goal of the project is to identify the cis regulatory properties that determine the specificity of enhancer-promoter interactions during vertebrate embryo development. The methods include transgenesis, reporter gene expression analysis in zebrafish embryos, molecular cloning, embryo phenotype analysis and bioinformatics. This studentship is an ideal opportunity to obtain skills and knowledge in several areas of developmental, molecular and cell biology and bioinformatics. Funded BBSRC Ph.D. CASE studentship

(The University of Sheffield and AstraZeneca)

Title: The zebrafish as an in vivo model to understand chemically induced hearing impairment.

Project overview:
Some compounds have a strong association with hearing (auditory) impairment, i.e. are ototoxic. However, the risk of ototoxicity in new chemical entities is not routinely investigated, mainly because of an absence of convenient methods of assessment. One potentially useful model, is this respect, is the zebrafish, which has previously been used to assess hair cell survival after compound exposure. Despite this, little is known of the precise nature of, or mechanisms leading to, the damage to hair cells in this species. More specifically, it is not known whether the observed ototoxicity parallels that seen in mammals, whether different classes of compound evoke pathological, biochemical and/or genetic responses in analogous auditory-system structures, and how such effects are manifested as alterations in auditory function.

This project aims to address this knowledge gap by using a range of ototoxins with known mammalian effects to probe equivalent effects in zebrafish. A range of techniques will be used including: detailed histopathological assessment of zebrafish inner ear and lateral line morphology; determination of effects on key pathways associated with ototoxicity in mammals; and functional assessment of auditory performance in zebrafish. The results obtained will be compared with existing models of ototoxicity, for example in vitro mammalian or avian hair cells, to comparatively assess model performance. Overall, this project will help to understand the mechanisms responsible for ototoxicity in zebrafish, and whether these mechanisms and pathological consequences are comparable with those observed in mammalian models of hearing impairment.
