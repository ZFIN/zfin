PhD Student

(Hubrecht Laboratory - Utrecht, The Netherlands)


The Hubrecht Laboratory, operated by the Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences, is a research Institute comprising 15 research groups of varying size. It is located in Utrecht, some 40 km from Amsterdam. In addition to a highly interactive research environment, excellent central research facilities are available, including housing for mice and zebrafish, and the presence of the invertebrate C. elegans. The Institute, directed by Prof. Ronald Plasterk and Prof. Hans Clevers, is located in the University campus De Uithof. It has close connections with the Utrecht University, such as the Graduate School for Developmental Biology in which the Hubrecht PhD students participate. Undergraduate students from Utrecht and other universities can spend their training periods in the institute.

In the group of Dr. D. Jongejan-Zivkovic at the Hubrecht Laboratory, a position is available for a

PhD Student (m/f)
full time (38 hours/week)

Description of the project: The PhD student will study the roles of Wnt/β-catenin signaling in patterning, proliferation, neuronal differentiation and axonogenesis in the developing zebrafish brain. These questions will be addressed in zebrafish embryonic and larval mutants that display ectopically activated or loss-of-function Wnt/β-catenin signaling as well as in compound mutants. While a number of mutants are already available, additional mutants will be generated using target-selected gene inactivation technology. The role of each of the mutated genes will be studied by marker-gene expression, differentiation-specific antibodies and proliferation and apoptosis markers. To define cell autonomous versus non-autonomous function of the mutations, cell grafting in embryos will be carried out. Identification of Wnt/β-catenin target genes involved in brain development will be studied by gene expression profiling (cDNA-based micro arrays) in different mutants.

Project financing: NWO-ALW

PhD Position

(NIOB/KNAW Hubrecht Laboratorium - The Netherlands)


PhD position at the NKI

The role of LKB1 in tissue morphogenesis and cancer in zebrafish.

The serine-threonine kinase LKB1 is a tumor suppressor in humans. Mutations in LKB1 lead to a gastrointestinal hamartomatous polyposis disorder with increased cancer predisposition, through a molecular mechanism that is not well understood. LKB1 has recently been shown to regulate polarity of human intestinal cells; its invertebrate homologues also function in cell polarity. Cell polarization has an important role in tissue morphogenesis and loss of cell polarity is an essential feature of neoplastic growth and metastasis. We have generated Lkb1-deficient zebrafish as a model to test the hypothesis that loss of LKB1 function may cause neoplastic transformation through disruption of epithelial polarization in a vertebrate organism.

The zebrafish provides a powerful animal model for genetic analysis of vertebrate embryogenesis, organ development and disease. Zebrafish have a short generation time (3 months), large progeny size (females typically lay 200 eggs at a time), the embryos develop outside the mother and are transparent. These characteristics make zebrafish an ideal system for dissecting developmental pathways and cellular mechanisms. The application of transgenesis, gene function knock-down by morpholino-modified antisense oligomers and the recent development of target selected mutational inactivation of genes, have provided a complete and powerful toolkit for genetic manipulation of zebrafish.

The project involves the study the cell polarization mechanisms during tissue morphogenesis and cancer employing Lkb1-deficient zebrafish. Analysis of cell-shape changes and cell behavior in vivo, will also be carried out.

Selected publications:

Haramis AP, Hurlstone AF, Wienholds E, Begthel H, Korving J, Van Eeden F, Cuppen E, Zivkovic D, Plasterk RH, Clevers H. The Wnt/beta-catenin pathway regulates cardiac valve formation. Nature. 2003 Oct 9;425(6958):633-7.

Baas AF, Kuipers J, van der Wel NN, Batlle E, Koerten HK, Peters PJ, Clevers HC. Complete polarization of single intestinal epithelial cells upon activation of LKB1 by STRAD. Cell. 2004 Feb 6;116(3):457-66.

Requirements:

We seek a highly motivated, enthusiastic PhD student with a strong interest in developmental pathways and cellular mechanisms during organ development and disease. The NKI provides a stimulating and ambitious environment. The candidate should have a University degree (Drs./MSc.) in biology or medical biology. The candidate should have experience in molecular biology techniques and ideally in genetics. Experience with zebrafish handling would be desired but not obligatory.

Available to start August-September 2005

Further information:

For additional information please contact Anna-Pavlina Haramis, email anna-pavlina@niob.knaw.nl
