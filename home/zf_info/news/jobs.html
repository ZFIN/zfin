Research Assistant Position

(The Rockefeller University) . Posted 27 Jan 2003

Back to top

A multidisciplinary research group that studies the cellular and molecular basis of hearing and balance is looking for a technician to run a state-of-the-art zebrafish facility with 1,200 tanks. The responsibilities will include the breeding and rearing of zebrafish, maintaining the facility, assisting with genetic studies, and training group members in aquaculture.

Skills required

The successful applicant must have a bachelor's degree in the biological sciences, preferably in marine biology, animal science, or aquaculture. Reliability, motivation, and strong organizational skills are necessary; prior experience with zebrafish husbandry would be a plus.

Research environment

The Hudspeth research group is investigating how hair cells, the sensory receptors of the internal ear, transduce mechanical inputs, such as sounds and accelerations, into electrical signals to the brain. Members of the group are employing anatomical, behavioral, biochemical, biophysical, electrophysiological, embryological, genetic, and molecular-biological techniques. The recent development of zebrafish as an experimental model system offers a unique opportunity to investigate the genetic basis of hearing disorders, which affect nearly one in ten Americans. The applicant will participate in an ongoing genetic screen of zebrafish for mutations that affect the ear and the lateral-line organ. All members of the group meet weekly to discuss research plans and results and to review recent findings from other laboratories.

Funded Postdoctoral Position

(University of Chicago) . Posted 10 Feb 2003

Back to top

Funded Postdoctoral Position to to study zebrafish endoderm regionalization in Vicky Prince's lab at The University of Chicago

A postdoctoral position is available to study zebrafish endoderm regionalization and pancreas specification in the Department of Organismal Biology at the University of Chicago. Our overall research program focuses on how the vertebrate primary body axis is regionalized during development. We have recently begun a productive study of the zebrafish endoderm (Stafford and Prince, 2002 Current Biology 12, 1215-1220), and now wish to expand this aspect of our program. Other ongoing projects in the lab are focused on Hox gene function during CNS regionalization (e.g. Prince 2002, Developmental Biology 249, 1-15; McClintock et al., 2002, Development 129, 2339-2354; Hunter et al 2002, Developmental Biology 247, 367-389). The Department includes two other groups studying the zebrafish, as well as groups working on insect, chick and mouse development, providing an excellent interactive environment for developmental biology research.

The successful applicant will be enthusiastic about developmental biology and have training in molecular techniques, embryology, or preferably both - ideally with some zebrafish experience. Recent PhD. graduates, or those close to graduation, are encouraged to apply. There is funding available for this position for up to three years (through a recently awarded grant from the Juvenile Diabetes Research Foundation), although the successful applicant will also be expected to apply for fellowships.

Postdoctoral Position

(IMCB, Singapore) . Posted 18 Mar 2003

Back to top

One Postdoctoral Position to study zebrafish liver organogenesis is available immediately in Jinrong Peng's lab at The Institute of Molecular and cell Biology (IMCB), Singapore. IMCB is a leading research institute in Asia and is fully funded by Singapore government (http://www.imcb.nus.edu.sg/).

Our goal is to identify genes involved in liver initiation and development in zebrafish. We have initiated a moderate scale of screening for liver defect mutants and ~50 putative mutants have been isolated. We have just started to clone these mutant genes via positional cloning method in hope to reveal how these genes are involved in controlling liver development. In addition, our lab has obtained ~15,000 unique cDNA clusters via our EST sequencing project. At the same time, we have setup microarray facility with optimized methods and conditions (Lo et al, 2003, Genome Research, pp455-466). We believe that the combination of genomic and genetic approaches will greatly facilitate our research program.

The successful applicant will be enthusiastic about developmental biology and have training in molecular techniques, embryology, or preferably both - ideally with some zebrafish experience. Recent PhD. graduates, or those close to graduation, are encouraged to apply.
